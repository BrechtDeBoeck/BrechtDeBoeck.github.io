My Abilities (SWOT)

STRENGTHS
I've got an eye for details, meaning I never leave a single requirement unaccounted for when working on projects.
I'm not afraid to ask questions if something might be unclear to me, whether it be course material, instructions or project requirements. If I'm struggling with something, I'm not one to shy away from asking help from the people with the right expertise.
I've an enormous willpower and perseverance, to the point where I continue to think of possible solutions during short breaks or free time. If there's a problem to be fixed, rest assured I'll do everything in my power to solve it.
Delivering a working, performant result which fulfills the client's requirements is my greatest priority. If I start slowing down or neglecting my planning, I always take initiative to set myself back on track. This can, however, go too far sometimes, due to my tendency towards perfectionism.
I find myself to be a fairly good planner. Barring any slip-ups every now and then, I have been the one to divide the workload between my various team members in the majority of all projects I have worked on, basing myself on each individual team member's expertise and what they themselves wanted to work on. I have tremendous amounts of respect for deadlines, and I organise my planning so as to meet each and every single deadline.
I often think of myself as a "cog in the machine" when working with a team. I put my best foot forward and make sure my share of the workload is done, and I expect my team members to be able to do the same.
English is like a second mother tongue to me: I have no problems when communicating with international colleagues.
I'm a creative person with a lot of ideas which could be useful if people want to listen to and use them. I'm always ready to take the initiative when designing a project.
WEAKNESSES
Even though I'm skilled at thinking of solutions for certain problems, documenting these solutions is a bit trickier. I often have trouble with converting information in my head into a physical form.
As mentioned before, I am very perfectionist: I often have the tendency to review my tasks multiple times over to search for any possibly left-over problems, bugs or features that could be improved or expanded further, even if the end result is already perfectly fine. People I've worked have already had to convince me to just let it go multiple times over.
I am most certainly not the person who wants to be in the spotlight. If anything, I prefer to be as far away from the spotlight as possible.
Giving presentations to higher-ups is within my skill-set, but not without buckets worth of stress. I prefer to leave this to people who're better at public speaking.
My code is mostly legible, but I find I need to take more initiative to provide documentation when needed.
Although I'm very confident in my abilities to develop both front- and backends for IT-applications, I'm less confident in my ability to secure said applications.
When an error pops up, it's often hard for me to find out exactly where the problem is coming from. In the end, I'm always able to eventually find the source of the problem and solve it, give or take a little online searching.
OPPORTUNITIES
There is currently a great demand for IT-people on the job market, in pretty much every sector. This means there's a great chance that I'm going to find employment when my studies have come to an end. Not only that, but industries for IT-applications (such as the growing video game-industry, or applications made to automate frequently executed business processes) are growing in importance. IT-people like me are guaranteed to have job security.
Because of Internet access and a big circle of friends in the IT-world, it won't be difficult for me to keep up to date with the latest developments in IT.
With all the tutorials and online seminars on the Internet, there is no feasible reason as to why I shouldn't be able to school myself even further after my studies have ended. And I am ready to take the initiative.
THREATS
The IT-businessworld is still relatively new to me, and it'll take me a while to fully adjust.
During economic crises, such as the Corona-crisis at the start of this decade, people might be less interested in investing in IT.
I'm still in my second year of college, so there's still a lot of subjects, including advanced security-techniques, that haven't been discussed in detail and which I'm not fully familiar with.
During my studies, I have been taught to favor Agile over other development strategies. This strategy puts the focus more towards delivering working software and less towards documenting said code. Because of this, I'm not too experienced in documenting my code and usually just assume others will be able to read my code as well. This has never harmed my teamwork, but I am aware of this risk.
